Image Gradients

Gradient in multivariate calculus is defined as a vector of partial derivatives, and the number of derivatives equals number of axes - 1.

If the gradient is the rate of change, when is it zero in an image? The answer could be in the background for example, where there is little change in a blue sky. The highest gradient appears at the edges, because this is when the pixel intensity changes drastically in a small window.

Gradients, like any vector, have a magnitude and direction, and their direction is perpendicular on the direction of change. So if we have a high magnitude gradient as a result of an edge, the gradients’ direction will be perpendicular on that edge.

Generally, sharp discontinuities in intensity result in high gradient values. These could be:

  • a change in color
  • in textures and patterns
  • or shadows

What can we use gradients for? We can use their properties in edge detection applications.

How can we calculate gradient for discrete values? We can approximation of derivatives

We have three methods of finding finite differences:

  • Forward difference
  • Backward difference
  • Central difference

Generally, central difference is the most accurate and so we use this one. Why does the Sobel filter blur first? The derivative filter takes note of discontinuities that could either be noise or an edge, so smoothing filter is applied first to remove the effect of noise. If change is consistent above and below, then it is not an outlier or artifact. This way we can be sure we are indeed marking an edge not a noisy part of the image.

Laplace Filter

If we take a second derivative, we get the Laplace filter

In the Laplace filter, we notice that zero-crossing occurs. What happens if we take Laplacian of Gaussian (LoG) filter (convolve the two filters)? We get a filter that looks like a Mexican hat. When we convolve this filter with an image, zero-crossing will occur at the edge.

What are the perks of this? The edge is now very thin, as very few pixels cross over! We are essentially localizing the edge based on a very specific criteria.

What are the downsides of this? It isn’t convenient to depend on the second derivative, since this requires more computational power.

Note that there is a difference between taking the Laplacian of the Gaussian filter and taking the derivative of it. Taking the derivative of Gaussian filtering is what we used to do before here, while taking the Laplacian of the Gaussian filter is convolving them together to get the new LoG filter. Image Pyramids and Frequency Domain

While usually when dealing with images we operate on the spatial domain, sometimes it comes in handy to explore the frequency domain as well.

Image Downsampling

This serves to reduce image size. Downsampling is done to decrease the bit rate when transmitting over a limited bandwidth or to convert to a more limited image format.

If we take the naive approach, we throw away half of the information by deleting even rows and even columns. This results in aliasing artifacts to start to appear.

Nyquist sampling theorem states that for downsampling to be done correctly, it must be able to be reversed. Otherwise the image cannot be reconstructed.

So how should we sample an image to adhere to this theorem?

Should we then take a very small number of samples?

Negative. Information will inevitably be lost.

Which signal was the original? Which frequency is correct? Since we cannot reconstruct the signal, this is undesirable.

The first example is called oversampling, while the second is undersampling. Undersampling often results in an aliasing effect.

How to deal with aliasing?

  • We can oversample → however, this requires too much computation
  • We can smooth the signal first. Why? Blurring acts as a low pass filter. Since all of our problems are caused by our inability to recover high frequencies, this should solve our problem. However, this will remove the small details in the picture that we may need.

Gaussian Image Pyramid

In the Gaussian image pyramid, we store all the qualities.

Its final total size will be the summation of a geometric series, so the size is only 1.33%, or 4/3 of the original image!

What happens to the details of the image as we continue with this loop and move to higher levels? The high frequencies are lost as they get smoothed.

Laplacian Image Pyramid

Here, we store the smallest image in the pyramid, then we store the difference between each of the bigger images, known as the residual.

Note that both the Gaussian and the Laplacian image pyramids take the same amount of space to store. This is because the residual image in the Laplacian gets stretched to the original size. However, if we were to somehow optimize the way it is stored, we can then reduce the size of the Laplacian pyramid.

There are also other types of pyramids, such as the steerable image pyramid, and wavelets.

What are image pyramids used for? Photo editing, image blending, multi-scale texture maping, focal stack compositing, multi-stack registration, image compression.

Frequency-domain filtering

The Fourier transform of the convolution of 2 functions is the product of their Fourier transform, which is great news because the multiplication operation is much less costly than convolution.

Back to the box effect the box filter had, what was the problem and how did the gaussian filter solve it?

The gaussian blur has much less frequencies in its output image than in the box blur. As a result, the box blur has undesirable artifacts.

Low pass filters have a smoothing effect as they don’t pass throw the high frequencies that signify sudden changes in amplitude. It will subsequently remove the edges’ sharpness.

Band pass filters remove some of the low frequencies and some of the high ones.

High pass filters remove the low frequencies while retaining the high ones, resulting in images that have the edges only.
